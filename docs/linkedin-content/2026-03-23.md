## Post 1 — Market Insight

**Headline:** Forty percent of enterprise apps will have AI agents by end of year. That's the problem.

Gartner now projects that 40% of enterprise applications will include task-specific AI agents by the end of 2026. That number is being celebrated as a milestone.

It shouldn't be.

"40% of your apps now have agents" means 40% of your existing SaaS sprawl just got more capable of acting on your behalf — inside systems you don't control, with logic you didn't write, producing outputs you can't audit.

The firms asking the right question aren't asking "which tools have agents." They're asking "what does it mean when five different vendors each run agents against my fund data, and none of those agents have a shared context about what the others are doing?"

That's not an AI problem. That's a systems architecture problem. And adding more agents to fragmented SaaS doesn't fix fragmentation — it accelerates it.

The REPE firms that will come out ahead aren't the ones with the most AI features. They're the ones that decided to own the execution layer before the vendors made that choice for them.

## Post 2 — Systems Architecture Commentary

**Headline:** NVIDIA just built an open agent platform. Enterprise buyers should read the fine print.

NVIDIA's new Agent Toolkit — announced this week with partners including SAP, Salesforce, ServiceNow, and Adobe — is a serious piece of infrastructure. The goal: give enterprises the ability to build and run self-evolving AI agents at scale.

The capability is real. The architecture question is the one worth asking.

When 16 major SaaS vendors all integrate the same agent runtime, each of them can now act more autonomously inside your workflows. That's genuinely useful, right up until the moment those agents need to coordinate — share context, respect authorization boundaries, or operate against a consistent data model.

MCP and similar protocol work is trying to solve this. Standardized schemas, reusable tool servers, cleaner integrations. That's the right direction. But a standard connector protocol doesn't tell you who owns the decisions those agents make.

The firms that will use agentic infrastructure well are the ones that treat it as a foundation layer in a system they control — not a feature they enable per vendor.

Capability without ownership is just faster dependency.

## Post 3 — Tactical Automation Example

**Headline:** LP reporting used to take two weeks. Here's what that actually means.

A REPE firm closes a quarter. The CFO needs LP reports out in 10 business days.

The old way: an analyst pulls actuals from the accounting system, pastes them into Excel, reformats by fund, checks waterfall calculations, builds the narrative section manually, exports PDFs, and emails them to the IR team for another round of review. Two weeks if nothing breaks. Three weeks if something does.

The new way: the execution environment already has the fund data, the waterfall models, and the LP-level reporting templates. A single instruction kicks off the generation run. The analyst reviews output and flags exceptions. Reports go out in two days.

The difference isn't the AI. It's whether the data, the logic, and the reporting layer live in one system — or across seven.

Fundrise just launched a public AI tool that surfaces market comps for retail investors. That's fine. REPE firms have different needs: fund-level accuracy, LP confidentiality, and zero tolerance for hallucinated returns.

Those requirements don't fit a general-purpose tool. They fit a domain-specific system the firm controls.
