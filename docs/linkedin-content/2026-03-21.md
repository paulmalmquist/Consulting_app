## Post 1 — Market Insight

**Headline:** Private Equity Is Starting to Ask the Right Question About Software

Private equity firms spent a decade buying SaaS companies on the thesis that software replaces headcount. Now they're staring at a harder question: what replaces the software?

CNBC reported this month that the PE software deal pipeline is largely frozen. Firms are re-underwriting their own portfolios because AI makes the unit economics of standalone SaaS products look fragile. Anthropic is in talks with Blackstone to embed AI directly into portfolio operations. The same firms that bought the tools are now looking to bypass them.

This is not an AI hype cycle problem. It is a systems ownership problem. When your operating workflows live inside a vendor's product, you inherit their roadmap, their pricing changes, and their architectural constraints. When AI arrives and that vendor pivots or gets disrupted, your workflows break.

The firms that will come through this transition cleanly are the ones building execution environments they own. Not buying another tool. Not adding a chatbot to the stack. Building the system that runs the business, with AI as the engine rather than the interface.

The question was never "should we use AI?" It was always "who owns the system where work actually happens?"

## Post 2 — Systems Architecture Commentary

**Headline:** The Real Cost of "Agentic" Is Showing Up in Architecture Decisions

Microsoft just shipped agentic business applications in their March Power Platform update. Zoom announced agentic workflow orchestration across their entire suite. Every major platform vendor is racing to add AI agents to their existing products.

Here is what none of them are talking about: where the state lives.

When an AI agent executes a task inside Zoom, the context stays in Zoom. When a Power Platform agent processes an approval, the audit trail lives in Microsoft's graph. When you string three vendor agents together to handle a single business process, you now have state scattered across three systems with three different retention policies, three different permission models, and three different failure modes.

This is the new version of tool sprawl. Instead of humans copying data between tabs, agents are copying data between APIs. The workflow looks automated. The architecture is still fragile.

A durable approach puts the execution environment in one place. The agents, the state, the audit trail, and the business logic all live in a system the firm controls. The AI layer reads and writes within that boundary. No state leaks across vendor lines.

Firms adopting AI agents this year should ask one question before signing anything: when this agent acts, where does the record of that action live? If the answer is "in the vendor's system," you have not automated your workflow. You have delegated it.

## Post 3 — Tactical Automation Example

**Headline:** One LP Report, Three Systems, Zero Reason It Should Take a Week

A mid-market REPE fund with 12 assets prepares quarterly LP reports. The process: pull financials from the accounting system, pull rent rolls from the property manager's portal, pull valuation assumptions from the internal model in Excel. Combine in PowerPoint. Send to IR for review. Two analysts. Four to five days.

The bottleneck is not the analysis. It is the extraction. Three systems, three logins, three export formats that never quite match.

In an execution environment, the fund's data already lives in one place. The AI layer reads the financials, rent rolls, and assumptions from a single source of truth. It assembles the narrative, flags variances against prior quarter, and produces a draft report in the firm's template.

Same two analysts now spend a morning reviewing and refining instead of a week assembling. The report is more consistent because the data pipeline does not change quarter to quarter.

This is not about replacing the team. It is about removing the part of the job that was always just moving data between systems that should have been connected in the first place.
