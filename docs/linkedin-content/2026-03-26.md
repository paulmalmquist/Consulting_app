Blackstone's BREIT just reported its first positive month of fund flows in four years.

That is not a small signal. Capital is rotating out of private credit and back into commercial real estate. The firms that pulled back on acquisitions in 2023-2024 are re-staffing, re-capitalizing, and getting ready to deploy.

Here is what that means practically. Deal volume is about to spike. Teams that spent two years in maintenance mode now need to underwrite faster, report to LPs more frequently, and manage portfolios that are about to get bigger in a hurry.

The question is whether the infrastructure is there. Most REPE firms I talk to are still running the same Excel-and-Yardi stack they had before the slowdown. That worked when deal flow was light. It will not work when you are trying to close three acquisitions in a quarter while also running quarterly LP reports across a growing portfolio.

The firms that use this window to modernize their data and decision infrastructure will compound the advantage. The ones that don't will spend the next cycle doing the same manual work, just faster and with more mistakes.

If your team is about to get busier, the time to fix the plumbing is now, not after the first LP call goes sideways.

## Post 2 — 40% of AI Agent Projects Will Be Canceled. The Reason Is Architecture, Not Ambition.

**[Revenue purpose]** Credibility building
**[Target segment]** All segments; reinforces Paul's "build it right" positioning vs. AI hype
**[Confidence: MEDIUM on Gartner stat]** — found via secondary aggregator, not direct Gartner publication `[REVIEW]`

Gartner is predicting that 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end of 2027. Cost overruns, unclear ROI, weak risk controls.

That tracks with what I see in the field. The pattern goes like this: a firm buys a horizontal AI agent platform, connects it to a few data sources, builds some automations, and six months later nobody can explain what the agents are doing, what they cost, or whether the outputs are trustworthy.

The problem is not that AI agents don't work. They do. The problem is that most firms are deploying general-purpose agent infrastructure into domain-specific workflows. A generic agent platform does not know what a waterfall distribution looks like. It does not know that your LP reporting has a different format for institutional vs. family office investors. It does not know that the cap rate assumption on your suburban office portfolio should be modeled differently than your data center exposure.

The AI projects that survive will be the ones where the domain logic was built in from day one, not bolted on after a failed pilot.

If you are evaluating AI tools for your investment operation, ask one question: does this tool already understand my asset class, or am I paying to teach it?
