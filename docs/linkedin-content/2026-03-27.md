**[Target segment]** B — REPE operators (Marcus Partners, Ardent Companies positioning), also attracts CEO/COO outreach from data center-focused funds

Data center deal volumes just surged 37 percent globally and 20 percent in the US. Hyperscalers are still building, and the capital that was stuck in private credit is now flowing back into real estate. If your fund has not already built a data center platform or expanded into that vertical, the window for moving first is closing.

Here is the operational reality. Data center deals are not like office or retail. Your underwriting model needs different assumptions for power capacity, lease duration, hyperscaler tenant concentration, and exit timing. Your LP reporting has to break out energy costs, nameplate power, and utilization scenarios. Your portfolio monitoring needs to track power grid stability and data sovereignty regulations that change by region.

The firms that move fast now will have a year or two to figure this out with higher acquisition volumes and rising capital availability. The firms that wait will be chasing cap rate compression in a crowded market.

If you are thinking about data center acquisitions, the time to build the analytics infrastructure is now, not after you have three deals in diligence and your finance team is drowning in spreadsheets.

## Post 2 — Your AI Team Sold You a Platform. What You Actually Needed Was a System.

**[Revenue purpose]** Credibility building + competitive positioning
**[Target segment]** All segments; positions Paul as builder not advisor; direct counter to Yardi Virtuoso and other horizontal AI platforms
**[Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH]** — Gartner stat sourced from secondary aggregator `[REVIEW]`, observed pattern is Paul's direct positioning opinion

Gartner is forecasting that 40 percent of agentic AI projects will be canceled by the end of 2027. The reason is always the same. Cost blew up. ROI did not materialize. Nobody could explain what the agents were actually doing.

I have watched this pattern repeat too many times. A firm buys an AI agent platform, plugs it into some data sources, builds a few automations, and six months later the project is dead. The platform does not understand your business. It does not know what a capital call looks like or what an LP prefers in a quarterly report. It does not know which line items on your balance sheet matter and which ones are noise.

The AI projects that actually work are not platforms. They are systems. A system has domain logic built in from the start. It understands your asset class. It speaks your language. It knows what success looks like in your operation because it was built inside your operation, not bolted onto it after the fact.

When you are evaluating AI tools for your business, ask your vendor this one question: did you build this to work in my industry, or did you build it to work in every industry and hope I figure out how to use it?

If the answer is the second one, keep looking.
