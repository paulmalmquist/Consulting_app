## Key Findings

First, the hyperscaler capex cycle is the most powerful demand driver in commercial real estate history. At 600 to 690 billion dollars in committed 2026 capex, with 75 percent targeting AI infrastructure, the demand for data center capacity is not speculative but backed by corporate budgets and customer backlogs. Microsoft's 80 billion dollar unfulfilled Azure backlog alone represents years of data center leasing demand.

Second, the cap rate spread compression to near zero against the 10-year Treasury is a significant valuation concern. Historically, when REIT cap rates converge with risk-free rates, the sector underperforms until either rates fall or cap rates widen. In the current RISK_OFF_DEFENSIVE regime with rates potentially rising further, this compressed spread creates meaningful downside risk even for fundamentally strong names.

Third, power constraints are the binding operational bottleneck. Data center project delays of up to 12 months due to grid power limitations represent both a risk and a competitive moat. Companies with existing power-secured capacity (like Equinix's operational portfolio) benefit from scarcity pricing, while greenfield development timelines extend, limiting new supply.

Fourth, the DLR-EQIX divergence is notable. DLR has outperformed the broader REIT industry by 12.5 percentage points over three months, and Deutsche Bank initiated with a Buy and top-pick designation. Meanwhile, EQIX faces overbought technical signals and some analysts are calling for a correction. This divergence suggests the market is differentiating within the subsector rather than treating data center REITs as a monolithic trade.

Fifth, the REIT sector broadly may benefit from a rate cut later in 2026. If the Fed delivers its projected one cut and rates drift toward 3.0 to 3.5 percent as BlackRock and others forecast by late 2026, the REIT dividend yield becomes relatively more attractive and the cap rate spread decompresses. Data center REITs with strong fundamental growth would likely lead a rate-driven REIT rally.

## Feature Gaps Identified

One, REIT fundamental screener. A screener that pulls AFFO, dividend yield, payout ratio, and cap rate for data center REITs (EQIX, DLR, AMT digital segments, CONE, QTS) would eliminate manual web searches for fundamental data. Integration with SEC EDGAR or a financial data API would enable this.

Two, cap rate spread tracker. An automated comparison of sector-level implied cap rates against Treasury yields, updated daily from FRED data, would provide the core valuation signal for REIT analysis. This is a high-priority cross-vertical feature bridging the trading platform to the REPE environment.

Three, hyperscaler capex tracker. A dashboard tracking announced capex commitments from the top five hyperscalers, updated quarterly from earnings transcripts, would provide the demand-side signal for data center REIT analysis. This could be built from SEC EDGAR 10-Q filings.

Four, power availability map. Mapping grid power capacity against data center development pipeline by geography would quantify the power constraint bottleneck. This requires utility data that may not be publicly available but could be approximated from EIA data and utility filings.

Five, REIT sector rotation model. A model that scores REIT subsectors (data center, industrial, residential, healthcare, retail) based on rate sensitivity, fundamental growth, and positioning would enable systematic sector rotation within the REIT allocation. This does not exist in the current platform.
