## Regime Context

The RISK_OFF_DEFENSIVE regime is being driven in large part by the rates and yield curve dynamics themselves. The Iran war that began in early March created an energy supply shock that is feeding directly into inflation expectations, which in turn is repricing the entire rate trajectory. The 2-year Treasury yield has spiked 53 basis points since early March as markets flipped from pricing one rate cut to pricing one rate hike. This is the most significant repricing of Fed expectations since the 2022 tightening cycle, and it is reshaping every asset class. In this regime, policy direction and rate trajectory signals carry the highest weights at 25 percent and 20 percent respectively.

---

## Signal Scores

**Policy Direction: 8 out of 10.** The Federal Reserve held the fed funds rate at 3.50 to 3.75 percent at its March meeting, the second consecutive pause. The March dot plot projects the target range declining to 3.00 to 3.25 percent by year-end 2027, implying only 50 basis points of additional easing over the next 18 months. However, the market is now pricing even less easing than the dots suggest, with the 2-year yield spike reflecting expectations of a potential hike rather than cuts. The Fed is caught between conflicting mandates: the Iran war is creating an energy-driven inflation impulse that argues for tighter policy, while the same war threatens to depress economic activity, arguing for easier policy. This policy tension is the defining macro story of Q1 2026. The next FOMC meeting on April 28 to 29 is expected to hold rates unchanged, but the statement language and press conference will be closely parsed for any shift in the inflation-versus-growth balance.

**Rate Trajectory: 7 out of 10.** Treasury yields have moved sharply higher in March. The 10-year yield finished March 20 at 4.39 percent, up from 4.15 percent on March 6. The 2-year yield is at 3.88 to 3.91 percent after a 53-basis-point spike in three weeks. The yield curve has normalized (uninverted) with the 10-year yielding 49 basis points more than the 2-year, modestly below the long-run average of 80 basis points. The normalization after prolonged inversion is historically a signal that recession risk is elevated, as the curve typically uninverts just before economic downturns begin. However, this uninversion is being driven by the short end repricing higher rather than the long end falling, which is more consistent with a hawkish repricing than a flight-to-safety dynamic.

**Liquidity Conditions: 5 out of 10.** Global liquidity conditions are mixed. The Fed has launched temporary reserve management purchases buying roughly 40 billion dollars in T-bills to maintain ample reserves, but these RMPs focus on short-term instruments and have minimal impact on the yield curve or broader financial conditions. The US government sold 606 billion dollars in Treasury securities in the week of March 21, a massive issuance week that reflects the ongoing borrowing needs of a federal deficit exceeding 7 percent of GDP. This supply wall is competing with risk-off demand for safe assets, creating volatile and choppy price action in Treasuries. M2 money supply changes lag central bank balance sheet moves by 6 to 12 months, meaning the current liquidity picture is still influenced by last year's policy stance.

**Cross-Asset Signal: 6 out of 10.** The rates move is transmitting across asset classes with high fidelity. Higher short-term rates are strengthening the dollar (DXY in the 99.5 to 100 range), which puts pressure on emerging market assets and commodities priced in dollars. The 2-year yield spike is tightening financial conditions beyond what the Fed intended, functioning as an endogenous tightening mechanism. Credit spreads, while still near historical tights with IG at 71 basis points in late January, are beginning to show signs of widening as the rate repricing filters through to corporate borrowing costs. The correlation between rates, equities, and credit is elevated, consistent with a macro-driven market where asset-specific fundamentals are being overwhelmed by the rates regime.

**Cross-Vertical Relevance: 8 out of 10.** The rates and yield curve segment has the highest cross-vertical relevance of any segment in the taxonomy. For REPE, the repricing of the rate trajectory directly impacts cap rate assumptions, mortgage rates (30-year mortgage rates have risen alongside the 10-year), and property valuations across all sectors. The uninversion of the yield curve is particularly relevant for REPE debt strategies that rely on floating-rate financing, as the short end repricing higher increases carrying costs. For Credit, the rate environment reshapes consumer lending economics: higher short rates increase deposit costs for banks and compress net interest margins, while the potential for rate hikes changes the risk calculus for variable-rate consumer loans. For PDS, the construction sector faces higher financing costs as the rate repricing feeds through to construction loan rates.

## Key Findings

**Finding 1: 53-Basis-Point Repricing in Three Weeks.** The 2-year Treasury yield spiked from approximately 3.38 percent to 3.91 percent in just three weeks beginning in early March, flipping market expectations from one rate cut to one rate hike. This is the fastest repricing of near-term Fed expectations since the March 2023 banking stress, and it was triggered by the Iran war driving oil prices past 100 dollars and reigniting inflation fears. The speed of this move suggests that many portfolios were caught offside with rate-cut positioning that now needs to be unwound. Source: wolfstreet.com

**Finding 2: Yield Curve Uninversion Signals Recession Risk.** The 10-year minus 2-year spread has normalized to positive 49 basis points after a prolonged inversion. Historically, yield curve uninversion has preceded recessions by 6 to 18 months across the last six cycles. However, this uninversion is being driven by short rates repricing higher rather than long rates falling, which creates a different transmission mechanism. The long-run average spread of 80 basis points suggests the curve has room to steepen further if economic weakness emerges and the long end rallies. Source: advisorperspectives.com

**Finding 3: Government Issuance Wall.** The US Treasury sold 606 billion dollars in securities in a single week, reflecting the enormous borrowing needs of a government running a deficit north of 7 percent of GDP. This supply pressure competes with safe-haven demand and creates volatile intraday price action in Treasuries. The issuance wall is a structural headwind for duration positioning and means that even risk-off flights to quality face supply absorption challenges. Source: wolfstreet.com

**Finding 4: Fed Dot Plot Projects Slow Easing.** The March 2026 dot plot shows the median FOMC member projecting the fed funds rate declining to 3.00 to 3.25 percent by year-end 2027, implying only 50 basis points of total easing over the next 18 months. This is significantly more hawkish than market expectations from just three months ago, and reflects the Fed's concern about inflation persistence. The dots project a low-3 percent terminal rate, suggesting the neutral rate has moved structurally higher in the post-pandemic economy. Source: bondsavvy.com

**Finding 5: Stagflation Risk from Iran War.** The Iran war has created a textbook supply shock: oil prices surging 25-plus percent, Strait of Hormuz disruptions removing 20 million barrels per day, and economists raising 2026 inflation projections while cutting growth forecasts. If Brent remains elevated through mid-year, global GDP growth could be depressed by 0.6 percent annually while CPI rises by over 1 percent. This stagflationary impulse is the primary driver of the rates repricing and the most consequential macro risk for all verticals. Source: weforum.org, morganstanley.com

## Feature Gaps Identified

**Gap 1 (data_source):** Cannot access real-time FRED API data for live yield curve readings. The source registry lists FRED with relevant series (DGS2, DGS5, DGS10, DGS30, BAMLH0A0HYM2) but we do not have programmatic access integrated into the research pipeline.

**Gap 2 (calculation):** No automated yield curve slope computation or regime classification based on curve shape. A pipeline that computes 2s10s spread, 3m10y spread, and categorizes the curve as inverted, flat, normal, or steep would automate a key input to regime classification.

**Gap 3 (visualization):** Cannot render interactive yield curve charts showing current shape versus historical comparisons. A visualization showing today's curve overlaid on the curve from 30, 90, and 365 days ago would dramatically improve brief quality.

**Gap 4 (cross_vertical):** No automated REPE cap rate sensitivity model linked to rate trajectory. The direct relationship between the 10-year yield and property cap rates is a high-value cross-vertical calculation that currently requires manual analysis.

**Gap 5 (risk_model):** No duration risk calculator for portfolio-level rate exposure. A tool that computes how a given rate move impacts a portfolio of fixed-income positions would enable actionable risk management recommendations in the brief.
